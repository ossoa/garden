# Schersmin (Mock Orange)

**Scientific name:** Philadelphus coronarius  
**Swedish:** Schersmin, Doftschersmin  
**Polish:** Jaśminowiec wonny

## Basic Information

Schersmin, also known as Mock Orange, is a beloved traditional Swedish garden shrub famous for its intensely fragrant white flowers. The scent, reminiscent of orange blossoms and jasmine, is one of the signature fragrances of Scandinavian summer gardens.

### Characteristics
- **Type:** Deciduous flowering shrub
- **Height:** 2-4 meters
- **Spread:** 2-3 meters
- **Growth rate:** Moderate to fast
- **Flowers:** White, 4-petaled, 2-4 cm across, heavily fragrant
- **Bloom time:** June (about 2-3 weeks)
- **Lifespan:** 40-50+ years

### Growing Conditions
- **Light:** Full sun to partial shade (more sun = more flowers)
- **Soil:** Adaptable; prefers moist, well-drained, fertile soil
- **pH:** 6.0-8.0 (tolerates wide range)
- **Hardiness:** Zone 4-8 (extremely hardy in Sweden)

## Location Notes

Schersmin is perfectly suited to the Stockholm area climate:
- One of the most reliable flowering shrubs for Swedish gardens
- Completely winter-hardy with no protection needed
- Thrives in the typical clay-based soils found in the area
- Plant near seating areas or paths to enjoy the fragrance

## Monthly Care Calendar

### January
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** This fully hardy shrub needs no winter attention

### February
- **Tasks:** 
  - Good time to plan any renovation pruning
  - Check for storm damage
- **Watch for:** Pruning cuts should wait until after flowering

### March
- **Tasks:**
  - Clean up any winter debris
  - Remove obviously dead branches (snap test)
  - Apply balanced fertilizer as growth begins
- **Watch for:** Buds beginning to swell

### April
- **Tasks:**
  - Water if spring is unusually dry
  - Add compost around base
  - Check for aphids on developing buds
- **Watch for:** Rapid growth begins

### May
- **Tasks:**
  - Enjoy the emerging flower buds
  - Water during dry spells
  - Stake any tall, floppy branches before flowering
- **Watch for:** Flower buds becoming visible

### June
- **Tasks:**
  - **Main flowering month - enjoy the fragrance!**
  - Water during dry periods to prolong bloom
  - Cut flowers for indoor arrangements
  - Plan pruning for after flowering ends
- **Watch for:** Peak bloom usually mid-June

### July
- **Tasks:**
  - **Prune immediately after flowering** (critical timing!)
  - Remove 1/3 of oldest stems at ground level
  - Thin crowded growth
  - Water during dry spells
- **Watch for:** New growth after pruning

### August
- **Tasks:**
  - Light trimming to shape if needed
  - Reduce watering as growth slows
  - Stop fertilizing
- **Watch for:** Strong new shoots developing

### September
- **Tasks:**
  - Allow natural hardening off
  - Water if early autumn is dry
  - Do NOT prune (would remove next year's flower buds)
- **Watch for:** Leaves beginning to yellow

### October
- **Tasks:**
  - Clean up fallen leaves
  - Add mulch layer around base
  - Last chance for deep watering before winter
- **Watch for:** Natural leaf drop

### November
- **Tasks:**
  - No protection needed (fully hardy)
  - Remove any remaining leaves if diseased
- **Watch for:** Nothing specific; plant is dormant

### December
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** Enjoy the architectural winter form

## Pruning Guide

### Understanding Schersmin's Flowering Habit
- Flowers form on **previous year's wood**
- New shoots produced this year will flower next year
- Pruning at the wrong time removes flower buds!

### When to Prune
- **Main pruning:** Immediately after flowering (late June/early July)
- **NEVER prune:** Late summer through spring (removes flower buds)
- **Exception:** Dead or damaged wood can be removed anytime

### Annual Maintenance Pruning
**Goal:** Renew the shrub by removing old wood and encouraging vigorous new growth

1. **Remove 1/4 to 1/3 of oldest stems at ground level**
   - Choose the thickest, grayest, most woody stems
   - Cut cleanly at soil level
   - This makes room for new growth

2. **Shorten remaining old stems by 1/3**
   - Cut to outward-facing buds
   - Remove weak or twiggy side shoots

3. **Thin crowded areas**
   - Remove crossing branches
   - Open up the center for light and air

4. **Shape the outline**
   - Cut back any wayward branches
   - Maintain natural arching form

### Rejuvenation Pruning
For old, neglected, or overgrown shrubs:

**Method 1: Gradual (Recommended)**
- Year 1: Remove 1/3 of oldest stems at ground
- Year 2: Remove another 1/3 of old stems
- Year 3: Remove remaining old stems
- Result: Completely renewed shrub, some flowers every year

**Method 2: Drastic**
- Cut entire shrub to 15-30 cm from ground in late winter (March)
- Will NOT flower for 1-2 years
- Produces vigorous new growth
- Full flowering returns in 2-3 years

### What NOT to Do
- Don't shear into a ball or box (destroys natural beauty)
- Don't prune in spring (removes flower buds)
- Don't neglect annual pruning (leads to sparse flowering)

## Seasonal Considerations

### Before Spring (February-March)
- Assess plant condition and plan pruning strategy
- Check for winter damage (usually minimal)
- Order fertilizer if needed
- This is NOT the time to prune (except dead wood)

### Before Winter (October-November)
- Schersmin is fully winter-hardy; minimal preparation needed:
- Clean up fallen leaves (prevents fungal overwintering)
- Light mulch around base benefits soil
- No wrapping or protection required
- Ensure plant is not sitting in waterlogged soil

### Maximizing Fragrance
- Plant near windows, doors, or seating areas
- Morning sun helps release scent
- Cut flowering branches for indoor enjoyment
- Plant multiple shrubs for a fragrance corridor

## Common Problems in Swedish Climate

### Pests
- **Aphids:** Can cluster on new growth; spray with water or insecticidal soap
- **Leaf miners:** Squiggly lines in leaves; mostly cosmetic, remove affected leaves
- **Voles:** May gnaw bark in winter; use tree guards if problematic

### Diseases
- **Powdery mildew:** White coating on leaves in humid weather; improve air circulation
- **Leaf spot:** Brown spots on leaves; remove affected foliage, clean up fallen leaves
- **Generally trouble-free** in Swedish conditions

### Environmental Issues
- **Poor flowering:**
  - Wrong pruning time (most common cause!)
  - Too much shade
  - Over-fertilization with nitrogen
  - Young plants (need 2-3 years to flower well)
- **Leggy growth:** Needs renovation pruning
- **Floppy stems:** Normal for some varieties; stake if needed

## Tips for Success

1. **Site for fragrance:** Plant where you'll enjoy the scent—near seating areas, under windows, along walkways

2. **Sun for flowers:** More sun = more flowers. Partial shade is fine but full sun is best

3. **Prune promptly:** Mark your calendar! Prune within 2-3 weeks of flowering ending (usually early July)

4. **Embrace the arch:** The natural arching habit is beautiful; don't fight it with shearing

5. **Cut for the house:** Flowering branches last well in water and perfume entire rooms

6. **Annual renewal:** Removing old stems each year keeps the shrub vigorous and floriferous

7. **Companion planting:** The green foliage is unexciting after flowering; plant with summer-blooming companions

8. **Heritage variety:** Philadelphus coronarius is the traditional Swedish garden variety; hybrids may have bigger flowers but less fragrance

9. **Allow space:** Don't crowd this shrub; it needs room to display its graceful form

10. **Propagation:** Easy to propagate from softwood cuttings in June-July if you want more plants

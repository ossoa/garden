# Saucer Magnolia

**Scientific name:** Magnolia × soulangeana  
**Swedish:** Praktmagnolia  
**Polish:** Magnolia pośrednia

## Basic Information

Saucer Magnolia is a hybrid magnolia known for its spectacular goblet-shaped flowers that appear in early spring before the leaves. It's one of the most popular ornamental trees in cultivation and reasonably hardy for Swedish gardens when properly sited.

### Characteristics
- **Type:** Deciduous large shrub or small tree
- **Height:** 4-8 meters
- **Spread:** 4-8 meters (often as wide as tall)
- **Growth rate:** Slow to moderate
- **Flowers:** Large (10-15 cm), cup-shaped, pink-purple outside, white inside
- **Bloom time:** April-May (before leaves emerge)
- **Lifespan:** 80-120 years

### Growing Conditions
- **Light:** Full sun to partial shade (sun promotes more flowers)
- **Soil:** Rich, moist, well-drained, slightly acidic
- **pH:** 5.5-6.5
- **Hardiness:** Zone 4-9

## Location Notes

Saucer Magnolia can thrive in the Stockholm area with careful placement:
- **Critical:** Avoid south and east-facing sites where early morning sun can damage frost-touched blooms
- **Ideal:** North or west-facing position with wind protection
- **Risk:** Late spring frosts often damage the early flowers
- The Stockholm archipelago's maritime influence helps moderate temperatures

## Monthly Care Calendar

### January
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** Check young trees for frost heaving; firm soil around base if needed

### February
- **Tasks:** 
  - Plan any needed care or structural pruning
  - Check for animal damage to bark
- **Watch for:** Flower buds swelling on warm days (don't be alarmed)

### March
- **Tasks:**
  - Apply balanced slow-release fertilizer as soil thaws
  - Refresh mulch ring (keep away from trunk)
  - Water if ground thaws and conditions are dry
- **Watch for:** Rapidly swelling flower buds

### April
- **Tasks:**
  - Enjoy the flowering! (Usually mid to late April in Stockholm area)
  - Protect blooms from frost if possible (fleece covers)
  - Water during dry spells
  - Watch for late frost warnings
- **Watch for:** Frost damage to open flowers (brown edges)

### May
- **Tasks:**
  - Remove spent flowers (optional, prevents seed formation)
  - Apply 5-8 cm mulch
  - Water regularly as leaves emerge
  - Any structural pruning should be done immediately after flowering
- **Watch for:** Leaf emergence; check for pest damage

### June
- **Tasks:**
  - Water deeply during dry periods
  - Monitor for pests
  - Remove any suckers from base
- **Watch for:** Scale insects on branches

### July
- **Tasks:**
  - Continue watering in dry weather (weekly deep soak)
  - Do NOT prune (flower buds forming for next year)
  - Keep mulch layer intact
- **Watch for:** Leaf scorch in very hot weather

### August
- **Tasks:**
  - Reduce watering frequency as growth slows
  - Stop fertilizing
  - Remove any dead or damaged branches
- **Watch for:** Powdery mildew in humid conditions

### September
- **Tasks:**
  - Allow natural leaf drop
  - Continue watering if autumn is dry
  - Plan any winter protection for young trees
- **Watch for:** Early color change in leaves

### October
- **Tasks:**
  - Clean up fallen leaves (compost if disease-free)
  - Add winter mulch layer (10 cm) around root zone
  - Water deeply before ground freezes
- **Watch for:** Premature leaf drop may indicate stress

### November
- **Tasks:**
  - Protect young trees (under 5 years) with burlap wrap
  - Ensure adequate mulch coverage
  - Install tree guards if rabbit/deer damage is a concern
- **Watch for:** Secure all protection before hard frost

### December
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** Heavy snow on branches; gently brush off to prevent breakage

## Pruning Guide

### General Principles
- **Magnolias dislike heavy pruning**
- **Never prune in autumn or winter** (poor healing, disease entry)
- **Best time:** Immediately after flowering (late May)
- **Wound healing:** Slow; avoid large cuts when possible

### When to Prune
- **Formative pruning:** Late May (young trees, first 5 years)
- **Maintenance pruning:** Late May (after flowering)
- **Dead wood removal:** Anytime, but late spring preferred
- **NEVER prune:** July-March (buds forming, disease risk, poor healing)

### How to Prune
1. **Remove dead, diseased, or damaged branches** at any time
2. **Shape young trees** by selecting a central leader or multi-stemmed form
3. **Remove crossing branches** that rub against each other
4. **Thin crowded interior branches** for air circulation
5. **Remove suckers** from base and rootstock
6. **Avoid large cuts** (over 5 cm diameter)—magnolias heal slowly

### What NOT to Do
- Don't top the tree
- Don't remove more than 25% of canopy in one year
- Don't prune after July (removes next year's flower buds)
- Don't leave stubs (cut to branch collar)

### Rejuvenation
Magnolias don't respond well to hard pruning. If the tree is overgrown:
- Remove 2-3 major branches per year
- Spread renovation over 3-5 years
- Or accept the natural form

## Seasonal Considerations

### Before Spring (February-March)
- Be ready with frost protection materials (horticultural fleece)
- Check forecast regularly during bud swell
- Clear any winter debris from around tree
- Ensure soil isn't compacted around root zone
- Order granular fertilizer if needed

### Protecting Spring Blooms
The biggest challenge in Swedish gardens is protecting early flowers from frost:
1. **Site selection:** Most important—avoid frost pockets and east-facing sites
2. **Microclimate:** Plant near buildings for warmth
3. **Fleece covers:** Drape over tree on frost nights (don't let fabric touch flowers)
4. **Accept losses:** Some years, frost will damage flowers—this is normal

### Before Winter (October-November)
- **Mulching:** Apply 10-15 cm of composted bark or leaf mold around base
- **Keep mulch away from trunk:** Leave 10 cm gap to prevent rot
- **Young tree protection:**
  - Wrap trunk with tree wrap or burlap (first 3-5 years)
  - Install windbreak if exposed
- **Hydration:** Deep water before ground freezes
- **Branch protection:** Tie up loose branches to prevent snow damage

## Common Problems in Swedish Climate

### Pests
- **Scale insects:** Brown bumps on stems; apply horticultural oil in spring
- **Aphids:** Occasionally on new growth; usually minor
- **Slugs:** Damage young shoots; use barriers or traps

### Diseases
- **Fungal leaf spots:** Remove affected leaves; improve air circulation
- **Verticillium wilt:** Serious; remove affected branches, no cure
- **Canker:** Prune out affected areas into healthy wood

### Environmental Issues
- **Frost damage to flowers:** Most common problem; site selection helps
- **Leaf scorch:** From hot sun or drought; water more, provide afternoon shade
- **Poor flowering:** Usually due to wrong pruning time or nitrogen-heavy fertilizer
- **Winter damage:** Protect young bark from freeze-thaw cycles

## Tips for Success

1. **Site is everything:** Choose a spot protected from east and north winds, away from morning sun
2. **Be patient:** Magnolias can take 5-7 years to flower reliably after planting
3. **Don't move it:** Magnolias resent transplanting; choose the location carefully
4. **Mulch, mulch, mulch:** Magnolias have shallow roots; mulch protects and feeds
5. **Water deeply:** Shallow roots dry out quickly; water thoroughly but infrequently
6. **Minimal pruning:** Less is more; magnolias naturally develop beautiful forms
7. **Accept frost losses:** Some years you'll have perfect blooms, some years frost wins
8. **Feed gently:** Use slow-release fertilizer; avoid high-nitrogen feeds
9. **Protect the bark:** Young magnolia bark is thin and easily damaged by mowers, trimmers, and frost
10. **Choose hardy cultivars:** 'Rustica Rubra' and 'Lennei' are more tolerant of Swedish conditions

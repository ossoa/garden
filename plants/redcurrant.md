# Redcurrant

**Scientific name:** Ribes rubrum  
**Swedish:** Röda vinbär  
**Polish:** Porzeczka czerwona

## Basic Information

Redcurrant is a hardy fruit shrub native to northern Europe, producing abundant clusters of bright red berries perfect for jams, jellies, juices, and fresh eating. One of the most reliable fruit crops for Swedish gardens, it thrives in the cool, moist climate of Scandinavia.

### Characteristics
- **Type:** Deciduous fruit shrub
- **Height:** 1-1.5 meters
- **Spread:** 1-1.5 meters
- **Growth rate:** Moderate
- **Flowers:** Small, greenish-yellow (April-May)
- **Fruit:** Bright red berries in hanging clusters (July)
- **Lifespan:** 15-20 years productive life
- **Yield:** 3-5 kg per mature bush

### Growing Conditions
- **Light:** Full sun to partial shade (some shade acceptable)
- **Soil:** Moist, well-drained, fertile; tolerates heavy clay
- **pH:** 6.0-7.0
- **Hardiness:** Zone 3-7 (extremely hardy in Sweden)

## Location Notes

Redcurrants are ideally suited to the Stockholm area climate:
- Completely winter-hardy with no protection needed
- Prefer cool summers—Scandinavian climate is perfect
- Tolerate partial shade (can grow on north-facing walls)
- The maritime influence reduces frost risk during flowering
- Traditional in Swedish gardens; well-adapted to local conditions

## Monthly Care Calendar

### January
- **Tasks:** 
  - Order new plants if expanding
  - Browse catalogs for new varieties
- **Watch for:** Plant is fully dormant; no attention needed

### February
- **Tasks:**
  - **Main pruning window** (before buds swell)
  - Remove oldest stems at ground level
  - Cut back side shoots
- **Watch for:** Buds may start to swell late in month in mild years

### March
- **Tasks:**
  - Complete pruning before end of month
  - Apply general fertilizer around base
  - Spread compost or well-rotted manure
  - Check for signs of big bud mite
- **Watch for:** Buds swelling and greening

### April
- **Tasks:**
  - Mulch around base (straw, compost, or bark)
  - Water if spring is dry
  - Watch for late frost during flowering
- **Watch for:** Small flowers emerging; bees visiting

### May
- **Tasks:**
  - Water during dry periods
  - Hand-weed around base (don't hoe—shallow roots)
  - Inspect for pests (aphids, sawfly)
- **Watch for:** Green berries forming after pollination

### June
- **Tasks:**
  - Water consistently (critical for good fruit development)
  - Net against birds as berries start coloring
  - Remove any suckers from base
  - Monitor for powdery mildew
- **Watch for:** Berries developing and starting to color

### July
- **Tasks:**
  - **Harvest time!** Pick when fully red
  - Continue watering during dry weather
  - Pick regularly to encourage continued ripening
  - Check nets are secure against birds
- **Watch for:** Berries ripe from early-mid July

### August
- **Tasks:**
  - Complete harvest by early August
  - Remove netting after final harvest
  - Water if weather is hot and dry
  - Post-harvest fertilizer (optional, low-nitrogen)
- **Watch for:** Late-ripening berries; clean up fallen fruit

### September
- **Tasks:**
  - Remove any remaining ripe fruit
  - Check plant health; note any disease issues
  - Plan pruning strategy
- **Watch for:** Leaves starting to turn yellow

### October
- **Tasks:**
  - Clean up fallen leaves (compost if healthy)
  - Add compost mulch around base
  - Good time for planting new bushes
- **Watch for:** Plant entering dormancy

### November
- **Tasks:**
  - New planting can continue until ground freezes
  - Winter pruning can begin
  - No protection needed (fully hardy)
- **Watch for:** Plant dormant

### December
- **Tasks:**
  - Winter pruning possible in mild weather
  - Plan purchases for next season
- **Watch for:** Nothing specific; fully dormant

## Pruning Guide

### Understanding Redcurrant Fruiting
- Fruit forms mainly on **2-3 year old wood**
- Some fruit on 1-year-old wood
- Wood older than 3-4 years becomes less productive
- **Goal:** Maintain 8-12 main stems of mixed ages

### When to Prune
- **Main pruning:** Late winter (February-early March) while dormant
- **Summer pruning:** Optional, late June-early July (improves air/light)
- **Avoid:** Late autumn/early winter (disease entry)

### Winter Pruning (February-March)

**Step-by-step annual routine:**

1. **Remove old wood**
   - Cut out 2-3 of the oldest stems at ground level
   - Choose thick, dark-barked stems over 4 years old
   - This makes room for new growth

2. **Thin new growth**
   - Keep 3-4 of the strongest new stems from base
   - Remove weak, spindly, or crossing stems

3. **Shorten side shoots**
   - Cut back side shoots (laterals) to 2-3 buds from main stem
   - This concentrates energy into fruit production

4. **Maintain shape**
   - Remove any stems growing inward or crossing
   - Keep an open, goblet shape
   - Allow light and air into center

### Summer Pruning (Optional, June-July)

**Benefits:** Better air circulation, earlier ripening, disease prevention

- Shorten all new side shoots to 5 leaves
- Remove suckers and low-growing shoots
- Improve light penetration to ripening fruit

### Training Forms

**Bush (most common):**
- Open goblet shape on short leg
- 8-12 main branches
- Easiest to manage

**Cordon (single stem):**
- Vertical or angled single stem
- Side shoots pruned to spurs
- Space-efficient, higher yields per area

**Fan (against a wall):**
- Branches trained in fan shape
- Good for north-facing walls
- Requires framework

## Seasonal Considerations

### Before Spring (February-March)
- Complete all pruning before growth starts
- Apply fertilizer around base
- Top up mulch layer
- Order netting if needed
- Check plants for signs of disease from previous year

### Pollination
- Redcurrants are self-fertile (one bush will produce fruit)
- However, cross-pollination improves yields
- Plant different varieties for better crops
- Early flowers can be damaged by late frost

### Harvesting Tips

**When to harvest:**
- Berries should be fully colored and slightly soft
- Pick entire clusters (strigs) rather than individual berries
- Easier to remove berries later with a fork

**How to store:**
- Fresh: Refrigerate for up to 2 weeks
- Frozen: Spread on tray, freeze, then bag
- Preserves: Make jam, jelly, or juice immediately

### Before Winter (October-November)
- Redcurrants are fully winter-hardy; no protection needed
- Clean up fallen leaves and fruit (prevents overwintering pests)
- Add compost mulch around base
- Good time to plant new bushes
- Check for coral spot on pruning wounds (pink/orange spots)

## Common Problems in Swedish Climate

### Pests
- **Aphids (Currant blister aphid):** Red blisters on leaves; usually cosmetic, spray with soap solution if severe
- **Currant sawfly:** Green caterpillars defoliate plants rapidly; inspect undersides of leaves, pick off or spray with Bt
- **Big bud mite:** Swollen, rounded buds; remove and destroy affected buds in winter
- **Birds:** Will strip fruit; netting is essential

### Diseases
- **Powdery mildew:** White coating on leaves and shoot tips; improve air circulation, remove affected parts
- **Coral spot:** Pink/orange pustules on dead wood; prune out affected areas into healthy wood
- **Leaf spot:** Brown spots on leaves; usually minor, clean up fallen leaves
- **Rust:** Orange spots on leaves; remove affected leaves, avoid overcrowding

### Environmental Issues
- **Poor fruit set:** Late frost during flowering; cover with fleece if frost expected
- **Small berries:** Drought stress; water more consistently
- **Sunscald:** Rare in Sweden; can occur on south-facing berries in hot years
- **Dropping fruit:** Bird damage or inconsistent watering

## Varieties for Swedish Gardens

### Classic Varieties
- **'Jonkheer van Tets':** Early, large berries, vigorous
- **'Rovada':** Late, very large berries, excellent flavor
- **'Red Lake':** Mid-season, reliable, good for processing

### Hardy Swedish Favorites
- **'Holländska Röda':** Traditional Swedish variety, reliable
- **'Rödpärl':** Sweet, good for fresh eating
- **'Rotet':** Large berries, high yield

### Special Varieties
- **White currants:** 'Vita Versailles' - sweeter, less acidic
- **Pink currants:** 'Rosa Holländer' - intermediate sweetness

## Tips for Success

1. **Net against birds:** This is non-negotiable; birds will take your entire crop in days

2. **Water for quality:** Consistent moisture = larger, juicier berries

3. **Mulch generously:** Shallow roots benefit from organic mulch; also suppresses weeds

4. **Prune religiously:** Annual pruning is the key to sustained production

5. **Don't over-feed:** Excessive nitrogen produces leaves at the expense of fruit

6. **Harvest promptly:** Ripe berries won't wait; pick every few days during harvest

7. **Partial shade is fine:** Unlike many fruits, redcurrants tolerate and even appreciate some shade

8. **Plant multiple bushes:** Even though self-fertile, 2-3 bushes give better yields and extend harvest

9. **Cordons for small spaces:** Train against walls or fences to fit more plants in less space

10. **Stagger varieties:** Plant early, mid, and late varieties for extended harvest (July through August)

## Recipes and Uses

### Classic Swedish Uses
- **Röda vinbärsgelé (Redcurrant jelly):** Essential accompaniment to game and meatballs
- **Saft (cordial):** Concentrated juice diluted with water
- **Kräm (fruit soup):** Traditional Swedish dessert
- **Fresh eating:** Sweet varieties like 'Rödpärl' are enjoyable fresh

### Preservation Methods
- **Jelly:** Strain juice, combine with sugar, cook to set
- **Jam:** Whole berries in sugar syrup
- **Juice:** Steam-extract or crush and strain
- **Freezing:** Excellent for later use; spread berries on tray first

### Nutritional Value
- Very high in vitamin C
- Good source of potassium and iron
- Contains antioxidants
- Low in calories

**Tip:** The high pectin content makes redcurrants perfect for jam-making; they also help set jams made with low-pectin fruits.

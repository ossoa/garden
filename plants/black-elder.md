# Black Elder

**Scientific name:** Sambucus nigra  
**Swedish:** Fläder  
**Polish:** Bez czarny

## Basic Information

Black Elder is a native European shrub or small tree cherished for its fragrant white flower clusters and dark purple-black berries. A plant of folklore and tradition, it's also valued for its culinary uses—elderflower cordial, elderberry wine, and jams are Scandinavian summer classics.

### Characteristics
- **Type:** Deciduous large shrub or small tree
- **Height:** 3-6 meters (sometimes taller)
- **Spread:** 3-5 meters
- **Growth rate:** Very fast (can grow 1-2 meters per year)
- **Flowers:** Creamy-white, flat-topped clusters (10-25 cm across), sweetly fragrant
- **Bloom time:** June
- **Berries:** Purple-black clusters, late August to September
- **Lifespan:** 60+ years

### Growing Conditions
- **Light:** Full sun to partial shade (sun improves flowering/fruiting)
- **Soil:** Adaptable; prefers moist, fertile, nitrogen-rich soil
- **pH:** 5.5-8.0 (very tolerant)
- **Hardiness:** Zone 4-8 (extremely hardy in Sweden)

## Location Notes

Black Elder is native to Sweden and perfectly adapted to the Stockholm area:
- Completely winter-hardy with no protection needed
- Thrives in the maritime-influenced climate
- Often found growing wild in hedgerows and woodland edges
- The fast growth makes it excellent for quick screening
- Be aware: it can become quite large and spread via suckers and bird-sown seeds

## Monthly Care Calendar

### January
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** Fully dormant; no attention needed

### February
- **Tasks:**
  - Good time to plan pruning strategy
  - Can take hardwood cuttings for propagation
- **Watch for:** Buds begin to swell late in month

### March
- **Tasks:**
  - **Main pruning window** (before leaves emerge)
  - Cut back hard if renovation needed
  - Remove dead or crossing branches
  - Fertilize with compost or well-rotted manure
- **Watch for:** Fast-emerging leaves

### April
- **Tasks:**
  - Mulch around base
  - Water young plants if spring is dry
  - Remove unwanted suckers
- **Watch for:** Rapid growth begins

### May
- **Tasks:**
  - Support heavy branches if needed
  - Continue removing suckers
  - Water during dry spells
- **Watch for:** Flower buds forming

### June
- **Tasks:**
  - **Harvest elderflowers** for cordials, fritters, etc.
  - Pick in late morning on sunny days
  - Leave some flowers for berry production
  - Water in dry weather
- **Watch for:** Peak flowering usually mid-June

### July
- **Tasks:**
  - Enjoy watching berries develop
  - Remove unwanted suckers
  - Light pruning to control size if needed (after flowering)
- **Watch for:** Green berries forming in clusters

### August
- **Tasks:**
  - Watch berries ripen (green → red → purple-black)
  - Net plants if birds are a problem
  - Water during drought
- **Watch for:** Berries ready late August to early September

### September
- **Tasks:**
  - **Harvest elderberries** when fully black
  - Cut whole clusters with scissors
  - Process or freeze immediately (berries don't keep)
  - Remove exhausted fruiting stems
- **Watch for:** All berries ripen within ~2 weeks

### October
- **Tasks:**
  - Clean up fallen berries and leaves
  - Add compost around base
  - Plan any major pruning for late winter
- **Watch for:** Leaves yellowing and dropping

### November
- **Tasks:**
  - No protection needed (fully hardy)
  - Can begin winter pruning in mild spells
- **Watch for:** Plant is dormant

### December
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** Enjoy the architectural winter form

## Pruning Guide

### Understanding Elder Growth
- Flowers and fruits form on **previous year's wood**
- Young wood is vigorous and produces best
- Old wood becomes less productive over time
- Elder responds extremely well to hard pruning

### When to Prune
- **Main pruning:** Late winter (February-March) before growth starts
- **Light trimming:** After flowering (July)
- **Avoid:** Pruning in autumn (less healing before winter)

### Annual Maintenance Pruning

**Goal:** Remove old wood, encourage vigorous new growth

1. **Remove dead, damaged, or diseased wood** (any time)

2. **Cut out 1/3 of oldest stems at ground level**
   - Select thick, gray, woody stems
   - Cut cleanly at soil level
   - Promotes vigorous new shoots

3. **Shorten remaining stems if needed**
   - Cut back by 1/3 to 1/2
   - Cut to outward-facing buds

4. **Thin crowded growth**
   - Remove crossing branches
   - Improve air circulation
   - Open up the center

5. **Remove suckers**
   - Elder spreads by root suckers
   - Pull or cut unwanted suckers regularly

### Hard Pruning / Coppicing
Elder tolerates and even benefits from coppicing:
- Cut all stems to 15-30 cm from ground in late winter
- Produces vigorous 1-2 meter regrowth in one season
- Will not flower/fruit that year
- Excellent for maintaining smaller size or rejuvenation

### Training as a Tree
To grow as a single-stemmed tree:
1. Select strongest central stem
2. Remove all other stems at base
3. Remove side branches from lower trunk (gradually, over years)
4. Allow crown to develop naturally at desired height
5. Keep removing basal suckers

## Seasonal Considerations

### Before Spring (February-March)
- This is your main pruning window
- Assess plant size and shape
- Decide on renovation level
- Clear any overwintering debris
- Order netting if birds stripped berries last year

### Harvesting Elderflowers (June)

**Best practices:**
- Pick on dry, sunny mornings (after dew, before heat)
- Choose fully open, fragrant clusters
- Avoid fading brown flowers
- Shake to dislodge insects before use
- Process within a few hours (or freeze)

**Harvest tips:**
- Leave some flowers for berry production
- Cut stems with scissors
- Don't strip the plant—leaves some for pollinators

### Harvesting Elderberries (August-September)

**When to harvest:**
- Berries should be fully black/purple
- Clusters may have some red berries still—these aren't ready
- Taste test: ripe berries are sweet-tart, not bitter

**How to harvest:**
- Cut entire clusters with scissors
- Remove berries from stems with a fork or fingers (or freeze and shake off)
- Wear old clothes—elderberry juice stains!

**Important:** Raw elderberries contain compounds that can cause nausea. Always cook berries before consuming.

### Before Winter (October-November)
- Elder is fully winter-hardy; no protection needed
- Clean up fallen berries (prevent unwanted seedlings)
- Add compost mulch around base
- Plan winter pruning work

## Common Problems in Swedish Climate

### Pests
- **Aphids (Elder aphid):** Common on new growth; usually minor, natural predators control them
- **Sawfly larvae:** Can defoliate plants; pick off or spray with insecticidal soap
- **Birds:** Will strip berry crops; netting is the only reliable prevention

### Diseases
- **Elder whitewash fungus:** White coating on bark; cosmetic only, no treatment needed
- **Leaf spot:** Brown spots in wet seasons; usually not serious
- **Verticillium wilt:** Sudden wilting; remove affected branches, no cure
- **Generally very disease-resistant**

### Environmental Issues
- **Too much shade:** Less flowering and fruiting; site in sun
- **Spreading/Suckering:** Can become invasive; regular removal of suckers needed
- **Self-seeding:** Bird-sown seedlings appear; remove unwanted plants
- **Wind damage:** Fast, soft growth can break; prune to control size

## Uses and Recipes

### Elderflower Uses
- **Elderflower cordial (saft):** Classic Swedish summer drink
- **Elderflower fritters:** Dip whole clusters in batter and fry
- **Elderflower champagne:** Naturally fermented sparkling drink
- **Tea:** Dried flowers make a relaxing herbal tea
- **Flavoring:** Add to gooseberry jam, vinegar, or desserts

### Elderberry Uses
- **Elderberry syrup:** Traditional cold remedy
- **Elderberry jam/jelly:** Deep purple, slightly tart
- **Elderberry wine:** Rich, port-like wine
- **Elderberry juice:** Dilute with water and sweeten
- **Pie/crumble filling:** Mix with apples or other fruits

**Safety note:** Always cook elderberries; raw berries, leaves, bark, and roots contain toxic compounds.

## Tips for Success

1. **Give it space:** Elder grows fast and large; plan for 4-5 meter spread minimum

2. **Sun for production:** Full sun = maximum flowers and berries

3. **Embrace the pruning:** Don't be afraid to cut hard; elder responds with vigorous regrowth

4. **Manage suckers:** Check monthly during growing season and remove unwanted suckers

5. **Harvest timing:**
   - Elderflowers: sunny mid-June mornings
   - Elderberries: when fully black, usually early September

6. **Net for berries:** Birds love elderberries; cover clusters with netting if you want to harvest

7. **Compost freely:** Elder is a nitrogen-lover; compost and manure improve growth

8. **Ornamental varieties:** Consider 'Black Lace' (dark leaves) or 'Black Beauty' for ornamental value with smaller harvests

9. **Traditional pairing:** Elder was traditionally planted near houses for protection (folklore) and because it attracts beneficial insects

10. **Wildlife value:** Even if you don't harvest, elder provides excellent food for birds and beneficial insects

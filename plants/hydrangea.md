# Hydrangea

**Scientific name:** Hydrangea macrophylla (and other species)  
**Swedish:** Hortensia  
**Polish:** Hortensja

## Basic Information

Hydrangeas are beloved flowering shrubs known for their large, showy flower clusters that bloom throughout summer. In Swedish gardens, the most common types are bigleaf hydrangea (H. macrophylla), panicle hydrangea (H. paniculata), and smooth hydrangea (H. arborescens).

### Characteristics
- **Type:** Deciduous flowering shrub
- **Height:** 1-2.5 meters (depending on variety)
- **Spread:** 1-2.5 meters
- **Growth rate:** Moderate to fast
- **Flowers:** Large clusters in pink, blue, purple, or white (June-September)
- **Bloom time:** Mid-summer to early autumn

### Growing Conditions
- **Light:** Partial shade to morning sun (protect from hot afternoon sun)
- **Soil:** Rich, moist, well-drained; high organic matter
- **pH:** 5.5-6.5 (acidic for blue flowers, alkaline for pink)
- **Hardiness:** Zone 4-9 (variety dependent)

### Flower Color and Soil pH
- **Blue flowers:** Acidic soil (pH below 6.0) + aluminum availability
- **Pink flowers:** Alkaline soil (pH above 7.0)
- **Purple flowers:** Neutral soil (pH 6.0-7.0)
- **White varieties:** Color not affected by pH

## Location Notes

The Stockholm area climate suits hydrangeas well, but variety selection is important:
- **H. paniculata** (e.g., 'Grandiflora', 'Limelight'): Most hardy, recommended
- **H. arborescens** (e.g., 'Annabelle'): Very hardy, blooms on new wood
- **H. macrophylla:** Less hardy; needs winter protection and sheltered location

The naturally acidic Swedish soil often produces blue flowers without amendment.

## Monthly Care Calendar

### January
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** Check winter protection is intact after storms

### February
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** Late winter is a good time to plan any changes or new plantings

### March
- **Tasks:**
  - Remove winter protection when frost risk decreases
  - Check for frost-damaged stems (wait to prune until April)
- **Watch for:** Emerging buds—avoid disturbing

### April
- **Tasks:**
  - Prune according to hydrangea type (see pruning guide below)
  - Apply slow-release fertilizer formulated for acid-loving plants
  - Add compost around base
  - Begin watering if spring is dry
- **Watch for:** Late frost can damage emerging growth—cover if needed

### May
- **Tasks:**
  - Apply 5-8 cm mulch (pine bark or compost)
  - Stake tall varieties if needed
  - Water regularly (hydrangeas need consistent moisture)
  - Apply aluminum sulfate for bluer flowers (if desired)
- **Watch for:** Aphids on new growth

### June
- **Tasks:**
  - Water deeply during dry spells (2-3 times per week)
  - Deadhead spent flowers on reblooming varieties
  - Apply liquid fertilizer mid-month
- **Watch for:** Flower buds forming; powdery mildew in humid conditions

### July
- **Tasks:**
  - Continue deep watering (hydrangeas wilt quickly when dry)
  - Deadhead to encourage continued blooming
  - Stop fertilizing after mid-July
- **Watch for:** Spider mites in hot weather; leaf scorch from sun

### August
- **Tasks:**
  - Reduce watering frequency as temperatures cool
  - Stop deadheading to allow seed heads to form (winter interest)
  - Do NOT prune—flower buds for next year are forming
- **Watch for:** Early autumn color development

### September
- **Tasks:**
  - Allow plants to harden off naturally
  - Continue watering if dry, but reduce frequency
  - Collect dried flower heads for arrangements if desired
- **Watch for:** First frost warnings

### October
- **Tasks:**
  - Clean up fallen leaves (can harbor disease)
  - Add extra mulch layer (8-10 cm) for winter protection
  - Water well before ground freezes
- **Watch for:** Prepare winter protection materials

### November
- **Tasks:**
  - For H. macrophylla: Install winter protection (burlap wrap, leaf cage)
  - For hardy varieties: Ensure adequate mulch coverage
  - Do NOT cut back stems (protects crown over winter)
- **Watch for:** Ground freezing—complete protection before this

### December
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** Check protection after heavy snow; brush off excessive snow load

## Pruning Guide

### Understanding Hydrangea Types

**Group 1: Blooms on OLD wood** (H. macrophylla, H. serrata)
- Flower buds form in late summer/autumn for next year
- Prune minimally; only remove dead wood in spring
- Removing stems = removing flowers

**Group 2: Blooms on NEW wood** (H. paniculata, H. arborescens)
- Flower buds form on current year's growth
- Can be pruned hard in late winter/early spring
- Pruning encourages larger flowers

### Pruning Schedule by Type

#### Bigleaf Hydrangea (H. macrophylla)
- **When:** After flowering (July-August) or early spring
- **How:** Remove only dead stems and spent flowers
- **Tip:** If winter-killed, cut to live wood; may not bloom that year

#### Panicle Hydrangea (H. paniculata)
- **When:** Late March to early April
- **How:** 
  - Cut back previous year's growth to 2-3 buds
  - Remove weak or crossing branches
  - Can be cut hard for larger (but fewer) flowers

#### Smooth Hydrangea (H. arborescens)
- **When:** Late March to early April
- **How:**
  - Cut all stems to 15-30 cm from ground
  - Thin out weak stems completely

### Rejuvenation Pruning
For old, overgrown hydrangeas:
- Remove 1/3 of oldest stems at ground level each spring
- Complete renewal over 3 years
- Or cut entire plant to 30 cm (H. paniculata and H. arborescens only)

## Seasonal Considerations

### Before Spring (February-March)
- Order any needed fertilizers (acid-loving formula)
- Check for winter damage but wait to prune
- Plan aluminum sulfate application if you want blue flowers
- Ensure winter protection can be easily removed

### Before Winter (October-November)
- **H. macrophylla (bigleaf) winter protection:**
  1. After first hard frost, surround plant with chicken wire cage
  2. Fill with dry leaves or straw
  3. Cover top with burlap
  4. Secure against wind
- **H. paniculata and H. arborescens:**
  - Thick mulch layer (10 cm) is usually sufficient
  - Leave stems standing for winter interest and crown protection
- Water deeply before ground freezes
- Do NOT fertilize after July (soft growth = winter damage)

## Common Problems in Swedish Climate

### Pests
- **Aphids:** Spring; spray with water or insecticidal soap
- **Spider mites:** Hot summers; increase humidity, spray undersides of leaves
- **Slugs/snails:** Damage young shoots; use traps or barriers

### Diseases
- **Powdery mildew:** White coating on leaves; improve air circulation
- **Leaf spot:** Brown spots; remove affected leaves, avoid overhead watering
- **Root rot:** From waterlogged soil; improve drainage

### Environmental Issues
- **No flowers:** Usually due to improper pruning or winter bud damage
- **Flower color wrong:** Soil pH; amend accordingly
- **Wilting:** Underwatering or root problems; water deeply
- **Winter kill:** Choose hardier varieties; improve protection

## Tips for Success

1. **Choose hardy varieties:** H. paniculata ('Limelight', 'Grandiflora', 'Vanille Fraise') and H. arborescens ('Annabelle', 'Incrediball') are most reliable
2. **Site selection:** Morning sun + afternoon shade is ideal; protect from drying winds
3. **Consistent moisture:** Hydrangeas hate drought; mulch generously
4. **Don't over-prune:** When in doubt, don't prune—especially bigleaf types
5. **Natural soil:** Swedish soil is often naturally acidic, favoring blue flowers
6. **Winter protection:** Essential for H. macrophylla; valuable insurance for all types in harsh winters
7. **Patience with bigleaf:** If it doesn't flower, wait—it may need time to establish flower buds

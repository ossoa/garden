# Forsythia

**Scientific name:** Forsythia × intermedia  
**Swedish:** Forsythia  
**Polish:** Forsycja

## Basic Information

Forsythia is one of the most reliable harbingers of spring, erupting in a blaze of bright yellow flowers weeks before its leaves appear. A tough, adaptable deciduous shrub, it's a beloved classic in Scandinavian gardens precisely because it thrives in cold climates and delivers a vivid splash of colour when the rest of the garden is still waking up.

### Characteristics
- **Type:** Deciduous shrub
- **Height:** 2–3 meters
- **Spread:** 2–3 meters
- **Growth rate:** Moderate to fast
- **Flowers:** Bright yellow, bell-shaped, appearing in dense clusters along bare stems
- **Bloom time:** Late March to April (before leaves emerge)
- **Foliage:** Mid-green leaves, sometimes with mild autumn colour
- **Lifespan:** 20–30+ years

### Growing Conditions
- **Light:** Full sun to partial shade (full sun = best flowering)
- **Soil:** Adaptable; prefers well-drained, moderately fertile soil
- **pH:** 6.0–8.0
- **Hardiness:** Zone 4–8 (fully hardy in the Stockholm area)

## Location Notes

Forsythia is well-suited to the Stockholm climate:
- Fully winter-hardy; no protection required
- Flowers best in full sun — shade reduces flower production significantly
- Tolerates urban conditions and exposed sites
- Three clumps at the top of the back garden form a bold early-spring feature
- Excellent at covering slopes or banks where mowing is difficult

## Monthly Care Calendar

### January
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** Plant is fully dormant; stems hold next year's flower buds

### February
- **Tasks:**
  - Force early bloom indoors: cut a few branches and place in warm water
  - Plan any structural pruning for after flowering
- **Watch for:** Buds may begin to swell by late February in mild years

### March
- **Tasks:**
  - Do **not** prune — flower buds are forming on bare stems
  - Water if snow cover has been absent and ground is dry
- **Watch for:** Flowers begin to open — stunning yellow display before any leaves

### April
- **Tasks:**
  - Enjoy peak flowering (usually mid-April in Stockholm)
  - **Begin pruning immediately after flowers fade** (see Pruning Guide)
  - Apply balanced slow-release fertilizer after pruning
- **Watch for:** Leaves start to emerge as flowers drop

### May
- **Tasks:**
  - Complete pruning if not done in April
  - Mulch around base (5–10 cm)
  - Water during dry spells while new growth is establishing
- **Watch for:** Vigorous new shoots growing from pruned wood

### June
- **Tasks:**
  - Water during dry weather
  - Remove any weak or crossing growth if needed
- **Watch for:** Full leafy canopy; stems growing strongly for next year's flowers

### July
- **Tasks:**
  - Last acceptable month for any pruning (light shaping only)
  - Water deeply during prolonged dry spells
- **Watch for:** Flower buds for next spring begin forming on new wood — **do not prune after mid-July**

### August
- **Tasks:**
  - Stop all pruning
  - Stop fertilizing
- **Watch for:** Flower buds are set; no further pruning or cutting this season

### September
- **Tasks:**
  - Enjoy any mild autumn leaf colour
  - Begin preparing for winter mulching
- **Watch for:** Leaves yellowing and starting to drop

### October
- **Tasks:**
  - Add winter mulch around the base (8–10 cm)
  - Deep water before ground freezes if autumn has been dry
- **Watch for:** Leaves fully dropped; flower buds visible on bare stems

### November
- **Tasks:**
  - No pruning, no fertilizing
- **Watch for:** Fully dormant; buds overwintering on stems

### December
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** Appreciate the architectural bare-stem silhouette against winter light

## Pruning Guide

### Why Pruning Timing Matters
Forsythia blooms on **the previous year's wood**. Pruning at the wrong time removes the flower buds that were set the summer before, resulting in a shrub full of leaves but no flowers.

### When to Prune
- **Main pruning window:** Immediately after flowering ends (late April to early May)
- **Light shaping:** Up to mid-July at the latest
- **Never prune:** After mid-July — flower buds for next year are forming

### Annual Maintenance Pruning

**Goal:** Encourage vigorous new stems that will carry next year's flowers.

1. **Remove dead, damaged, or diseased wood** — cut to the base or to healthy growth

2. **Cut out 1/3 of the oldest stems at ground level**
   - Target the thickest, woodiest stems
   - Removing old wood forces strong new growth from the base

3. **Shorten remaining stems by up to 1/3**
   - Cut just above a strong outward-facing bud or lateral shoot
   - This keeps the shrub shapely and prevents it from getting leggy

4. **Thin crowded growth**
   - Remove branches that cross or rub
   - Open up the centre to improve air circulation

### Renovation Pruning
If a forsythia has become overgrown and woody with poor flowering:
- Cut all stems back hard to 30–45 cm from ground level immediately after flowering
- The plant will not flower next year but will produce vigorous new growth
- Resume normal annual pruning from year two

## Seasonal Considerations

### Before Spring (January–March)
- No action required; do not prune
- Flower buds are visible on bare stems — a good sign of a healthy plant
- Forced branches in a vase indoors are a beautiful late-winter treat

### Flowering Season (March–April)
- Peak display usually lasts 2–3 weeks
- No tasks needed during bloom; simply enjoy it
- As soon as the last flowers fade, pruning can begin

### Summer (June–July)
- The shrub grows vigorously and sets flower buds for next year
- Water during extended dry periods
- No pruning after mid-July

### Before Winter (October–November)
- Forsythia is fully hardy in Stockholm — no special protection needed
- Add a layer of compost mulch around the base for soil health
- No pruning

## Common Problems in Swedish Climate

### Pests
- **Aphids:** Occasionally on new shoots; usually minor and self-correcting
- **Gall mites:** Can cause distorted buds; remove affected stems
- **Generally pest-resistant**

### Diseases
- **Crown gall:** Rough growths at soil level; remove affected stems and avoid damaging roots
- **Leaf spot:** Minor cosmetic issue in wet summers; no treatment usually needed
- **Dieback:** Often pruning-related; cut back to healthy wood

### Environmental Issues
- **No flowers:** Most common cause is pruning at the wrong time (removing summer-set buds). Prune only right after flowering.
- **Leggy, open habit:** Insufficient pruning over several years; use renovation pruning to reset
- **Reduced flowering in shade:** Relocate or thin surrounding plants to improve light

## Tips for Success

1. **Prune right after flowering** — this is the single most important rule for forsythia; get the timing wrong and you lose next year's display

2. **Full sun matters** — forsythia in shade produces far fewer flowers; position or thin surrounding plants accordingly

3. **Don't be shy with pruning** — the harder you prune (within the correct window), the more vigorous the new flowering stems

4. **Avoid autumn pruning** — it may look tidy but removes the buds that hold next year's colour

5. **Forced branches:** Cut a few stems in February, place in a vase of warm water indoors, and enjoy bright yellow flowers while the garden is still bare

6. **Mulch annually** — a spring mulch of compost after pruning feeds the soil and retains moisture during the summer growth period

7. **Group plantings** — the three clumps in the top garden create a bold visual impact; keep them roughly even in size through consistent annual pruning

# Boxwood

**Scientific name:** Buxus sempervirens  
**Swedish:** Buxbom  
**Polish:** Bukszpan

## Basic Information

Boxwood is a classic evergreen shrub that has been cultivated for centuries in European gardens. Valued for its dense, fine-textured foliage and tolerance of clipping, it's traditionally used for hedges, edging, and topiary. In Swedish gardens, winter hardiness and proper variety selection are crucial.

### Characteristics
- **Type:** Evergreen shrub
- **Height:** 1-5 meters (often kept smaller by trimming)
- **Spread:** 1-3 meters
- **Growth rate:** Slow (10-15 cm per year)
- **Flowers:** Inconspicuous, yellowish-green (April)
- **Foliage:** Small, glossy, dark green leaves year-round
- **Lifespan:** Extremely long-lived (100+ years)

### Growing Conditions
- **Light:** Full sun to partial shade (prefers some afternoon shade)
- **Soil:** Well-drained, fertile, slightly alkaline to neutral
- **pH:** 6.5-7.5
- **Hardiness:** Zone 5-8 (variety dependent; choose hardy types for Sweden)

## Location Notes

Boxwood can grow in the Stockholm area but requires attention:
- **Choose hardy varieties:** 'Handsworthiensis', 'Rotundifolia', or Korean boxwood (B. sinica var. insularis)
- **Winter challenge:** Cold, drying winds and winter sun cause bronzing
- **Ideal location:** Protected spot, afternoon shade, sheltered from north/east winds
- **Container growing:** Common in Sweden; allows moving to protected areas in winter

## Monthly Care Calendar

### January
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** Winter bronzing of foliage (usually recovers in spring)

### February
- **Tasks:**
  - Check for snow damage; gently brush off heavy loads
  - Water container plants on mild days if soil is dry
- **Watch for:** Desiccation damage from cold, dry winds

### March
- **Tasks:**
  - Remove any winter protection
  - Check for dead branches (wait to prune until late spring)
  - Start watering container plants as weather warms
- **Watch for:** New growth emerging; potential frost damage

### April
- **Tasks:**
  - Apply balanced slow-release fertilizer
  - Begin regular watering
  - Check for boxwood blight (see disease section)
  - Light trimming of dead tips
- **Watch for:** Inconspicuous flowering attracts bees

### May
- **Tasks:**
  - First trim of the season (mid to late May)
  - Water during dry periods
  - Mulch around base (keep away from stems)
- **Watch for:** Box tree moth caterpillars (see pests)

### June
- **Tasks:**
  - Main shaping/clipping as needed
  - Water consistently (shallow roots dry out quickly)
  - Monitor closely for pests
  - Liquid feed if growth seems slow
- **Watch for:** Boxwood blight in humid conditions

### July
- **Tasks:**
  - Second trim for formal shapes
  - Water regularly during hot, dry weather
  - Check inside dense growth for webbing (moth larvae)
- **Watch for:** Heat stress; move containers to afternoon shade

### August
- **Tasks:**
  - Final trim of the season (before mid-August)
  - Continue watering in dry weather
  - Stop fertilizing
- **Watch for:** New growth hardening off

### September
- **Tasks:**
  - No more trimming (new growth won't harden before winter)
  - Reduce watering as temperatures cool
  - Prepare winter protection materials
- **Watch for:** Consistent green color = healthy plant

### October
- **Tasks:**
  - Deep water before ground freezes
  - Apply winter mulch around base (5-8 cm)
  - Move container plants to sheltered locations
  - Clean up fallen leaves around plants
- **Watch for:** Plan protection strategy for exposed plants

### November
- **Tasks:**
  - Install winter protection for exposed plants:
    - Burlap windbreaks
    - Shade cloth on south side
  - Tie up loose branches to prevent snow damage
  - Ensure containers are elevated (drainage)
- **Watch for:** Secure protection before hard frost

### December
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** Brush off heavy wet snow; check protection after storms

## Pruning Guide

### Trimming vs. Pruning
- **Trimming:** Light clipping for shape maintenance
- **Pruning:** Removing larger branches for renovation or health

### When to Trim
- **First trim:** Late May (after main spring growth)
- **Second trim:** Mid-July (if needed for formal shapes)
- **Third trim:** Early August (last trim of season)
- **Stop trimming:** After mid-August (new growth won't harden)

### How to Trim
1. Use sharp hedge shears or hand shears
2. Trim on an overcast day (fresh cuts can scorch in sun)
3. Remove no more than 5-10 cm per trimming
4. Angle cuts slightly so top is narrower than base (light reaches all sides)
5. Clean up clippings inside the plant (prevents moisture/pest problems)

### Trimming Formal Shapes
- **Hedges:** Maintain trapezoidal profile (wider at base)
- **Balls/Spheres:** Use templates or frame guides
- **Topiary:** Frequent light trimming better than infrequent heavy cuts
- **Timing:** More frequent trimming (3x/year) creates denser growth

### Renovation Pruning
For overgrown or damaged boxwood:
- **When:** Late spring (May)
- **How:**
  - Can be cut back hard into old wood
  - Cut to 15-30 cm above ground if needed
  - Will regenerate (slowly) from old wood
  - Takes 3-5 years to regain full form
- **Staged approach:** Cut back 1/3 of plant each year for 3 years

### Removing Winter Damage
- Wait until late May to assess
- Cut damaged portions back to healthy green growth
- Feed and water well to encourage recovery

## Seasonal Considerations

### Before Spring (February-March)
- Assess winter damage but don't prune yet
- Check stored container plants
- Order fertilizer and any replacement plants
- Plan pest monitoring strategy

### The Box Tree Moth Threat
**Cydalima perspectalis** has spread to Scandinavia:
- Check regularly from May-September
- Look for webbing and green caterpillars inside shrubs
- Treat promptly with Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) or neem
- Pheromone traps help monitor populations

### Before Winter (October-November)

**Preventing Winter Damage:**

1. **Hydration is critical**
   - Water deeply before ground freezes
   - Desiccation (drying out) is the main winter killer
   - Roots can't take up water from frozen soil

2. **Wind protection**
   - North and east winds cause worst damage
   - Install burlap screens on windward side
   - Or plant permanent evergreen windbreak

3. **Sun protection**
   - Paradoxically, south/west exposure causes problems
   - Warm sun on frozen soil = moisture loss
   - Shade cloth or burlap on sunny side helps

4. **Snow management**
   - Tie up branches loosely to prevent breakage
   - Heavy wet snow is the enemy
   - Don't let snow pile up on top

5. **Container care**
   - Move to sheltered location
   - Insulate pots with bubble wrap
   - Elevate for drainage
   - Water occasionally during mild spells

## Common Problems in Swedish Climate

### Pests
- **Box tree moth:** Devastating; caterpillars skeletonize plants. Check weekly May-September; treat with Bt
- **Box psyllid:** Curled, cupped leaves. Remove affected growth
- **Spider mites:** In hot, dry conditions. Spray with water
- **Boxwood leafminer:** Blistered leaves. Remove affected growth

### Diseases
- **Boxwood blight (Cylindrocladium/Calonectria):** Black spots on leaves, black streaks on stems. Remove and destroy affected plants; no cure
- **Volutella blight:** Pink spore masses, dieback. Prune out affected areas; improve air circulation
- **Root rot:** From waterlogged soil. Ensure good drainage

### Environmental Issues
- **Winter bronzing:** Leaves turn bronze/brown in winter sun. Usually recovers in spring; reduce with winter protection
- **Die-back:** Often from desiccation or disease. Cut back to healthy wood
- **Salt damage:** Brown, scorched leaves from road salt. Flush soil with water in spring

## Tips for Success

1. **Choose the right variety:**
   - 'Handsworthiensis' - most winter-hardy
   - 'Rotundifolia' - good hardiness, rounded leaves
   - Korean boxwood (B. sinica var. insularis) - exceptionally cold tolerant
   - Avoid less hardy types like 'Suffruticosa' for exposed locations

2. **Location matters:**
   - Sheltered from wind, especially north/east
   - Afternoon shade prevents winter sun damage
   - Good drainage essential
   - Away from road salt splash

3. **Container success:**
   - Use large containers (minimum 30 cm diameter)
   - Ensure excellent drainage
   - Move to sheltered spot for winter
   - Insulate pots against deep freezing

4. **Watering wisdom:**
   - Shallow roots need consistent moisture
   - Water deeply but not constantly
   - Critical: water before winter freeze
   - Don't let containers dry out in winter

5. **Pest vigilance:**
   - Check weekly for box tree moth from May
   - Early detection = easier treatment
   - Consider pheromone traps for monitoring

6. **Disease prevention:**
   - Ensure good air circulation
   - Don't clip in wet weather
   - Clean tools between plants
   - Remove and destroy infected material

7. **Feeding program:**
   - Spring: slow-release balanced fertilizer
   - Summer: optional liquid feed
   - Autumn: stop feeding (tender growth vulnerable to frost)

8. **Patience with formality:**
   - Dense formal shapes take years to develop
   - Frequent light trims better than heavy cuts
   - Slow growth = long-term structural plants

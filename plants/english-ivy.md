# English Ivy

**Scientific name:** Hedera helix  
**Swedish:** Murgröna  
**Polish:** Bluszcz pospolity

## Basic Information

English Ivy is an evergreen climbing and ground-cover plant native to Europe. It's one of the hardiest evergreen climbers suitable for Swedish gardens, capable of withstanding temperatures down to -25°C when established.

### Characteristics
- **Type:** Evergreen climber / ground cover
- **Height:** Up to 20-30 meters when climbing
- **Spread:** Indefinite as ground cover
- **Growth rate:** Moderate to fast once established
- **Lifespan:** Very long-lived (can survive for centuries)
- **Flowers:** Small greenish-yellow (autumn, on mature plants)
- **Berries:** Black, appearing in late winter/spring (mildly toxic)

### Growing Conditions
- **Light:** Full shade to partial sun (tolerates deep shade)
- **Soil:** Adaptable; prefers moist, well-drained, humus-rich soil
- **pH:** 6.0-7.5 (slightly acidic to neutral)
- **Hardiness:** Zone 4-9 (very hardy in Stockholm area)

## Location Notes

English Ivy thrives in the maritime-influenced climate of the Stockholm area. The relatively mild winters (compared to inland Sweden) are ideal. However:
- Young plants may need winter protection in their first 1-2 years
- North-facing walls provide ideal conditions
- Can suffer from winter sun scorch on south-facing walls if soil is frozen

## Monthly Care Calendar

### January
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** Heavy snow accumulation on climbing stems can cause breakage; gently brush off if excessive

### February
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** Late winter sun scorch on exposed leaves; water if ground thaws and soil is dry

### March
- **Tasks:** Light cleanup of dead or damaged leaves
- **Watch for:** New growth beginning; check ties and supports

### April
- **Tasks:** 
  - Begin regular watering if spring is dry
  - Apply balanced fertilizer (10-10-10) around base
  - Remove any winter-damaged stems
- **Watch for:** Aphids on new growth

### May
- **Tasks:**
  - First trim of the season if needed to control spread
  - Direct new growth onto supports
  - Mulch around base to retain moisture
- **Watch for:** Vigorous growth begins; keep away from gutters and windows

### June
- **Tasks:**
  - Trim to control size and shape
  - Water during dry periods
  - Check for spider mites in hot weather
- **Watch for:** Growth accelerates significantly

### July
- **Tasks:**
  - Continue trimming as needed
  - Water deeply during dry spells
  - Keep trimmed away from roof edges and window frames
- **Watch for:** Bacterial leaf spot in humid conditions

### August
- **Tasks:**
  - Last major trim of the season (before September)
  - Reduce watering if weather cools
- **Watch for:** Slowing growth rate

### September
- **Tasks:**
  - Light trimming only (avoid stimulating new growth before winter)
  - Stop fertilizing
- **Watch for:** Mature plants may begin flowering

### October
- **Tasks:**
  - Clean up fallen leaves around base
  - Check wall attachments are secure before winter
  - Add mulch for winter protection (young plants)
- **Watch for:** Berry formation on mature plants

### November
- **Tasks:**
  - Protect young plants (first 2 winters) with burlap or fleece
  - Water if autumn has been dry (before ground freezes)
- **Watch for:** Ensure good drainage to prevent root rot over winter

### December
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** Check protective coverings after storms

## Pruning Guide

### When to Prune
- **Main pruning:** Late spring (May) and mid-summer (July)
- **Light trimming:** Anytime during growing season
- **Avoid:** Pruning after August to prevent tender new growth before winter

### How to Prune
1. Use sharp, clean secateurs or hedge shears
2. Cut back to a leaf node or main stem
3. Remove any stems growing in unwanted directions
4. Thin out congested growth to improve air circulation
5. Cut back from windows, gutters, and roof edges by at least 30 cm

### Rejuvenation Pruning
If ivy becomes overgrown or woody:
- Can be cut back hard in late spring (May)
- Cut to 30-60 cm from the base
- Will regrow vigorously within 2-3 seasons
- Best done over 2-3 years to maintain some coverage

## Seasonal Considerations

### Before Spring (February-March)
- Inspect for winter damage
- Check wall attachments and supports
- Plan any major pruning or removal work
- Order fertilizer

### Before Winter (October-November)
- Complete all pruning by end of August
- Ensure plants are well-watered before freeze
- Protect young plants with fleece or burlap
- Secure loose stems to prevent wind damage
- Add 5-10 cm mulch around base of young plants

## Common Problems in Swedish Climate

### Pests
- **Aphids:** Common in spring; spray with water or use insecticidal soap
- **Spider mites:** In hot, dry summers; increase humidity
- **Scale insects:** Remove by hand or use horticultural oil

### Diseases
- **Bacterial leaf spot:** Remove affected leaves; improve air circulation
- **Root rot:** Ensure good drainage; avoid overwatering

### Environmental Issues
- **Winter burn:** Brown, dried leaves from winter sun/wind; usually recovers
- **Slow establishment:** Normal in first 2 years; be patient

---

## Tips for Success

1. **Choose the right variety:** 'Baltica' is exceptionally hardy and recommended for Swedish gardens
2. **Plant in spring:** Gives roots time to establish before winter
3. **Provide support:** Use trellis, wires, or let it climb on masonry (avoid wooden structures)
4. **Be patient:** Growth is slow in the first 2 years, then accelerates
5. **Control spread:** Regular trimming prevents ivy from becoming invasive
6. **Wall considerations:** Can damage old mortar; inspect walls annually

# Rhododendron

**Scientific name:** Rhododendron spp.  
**Swedish:** Rododendron  
**Polish:** Rododendron (Różanecznik)

## Basic Information

Rhododendrons are evergreen flowering shrubs prized for their spectacular spring blooms and year-round foliage. The genus includes over 1,000 species, ranging from dwarf alpine varieties to tree-sized specimens. For Swedish gardens, selecting winter-hardy cultivars is essential.

### Characteristics
- **Type:** Evergreen shrub
- **Height:** 0.5-4 meters (depending on variety)
- **Spread:** 0.5-4 meters
- **Growth rate:** Slow to moderate
- **Flowers:** Clusters of trumpet-shaped blooms in white, pink, red, purple, or yellow
- **Bloom time:** Late April to early June
- **Foliage:** Large, leathery, dark green leaves (year-round)

### Growing Conditions
- **Light:** Partial shade (dappled light ideal); protect from hot afternoon sun
- **Soil:** Acidic, moist, well-drained, high in organic matter
- **pH:** 4.5-6.0 (critical—rhododendrons cannot tolerate alkaline soil)
- **Hardiness:** Zone 4-8 (variety dependent)

## Location Notes

Rhododendrons can thrive in the Stockholm area with proper care:
- **Soil advantage:** Swedish forest soils are naturally acidic—often perfect for rhododendrons
- **Winter challenge:** Cold, drying winds are the main threat
- **Ideal site:** Under light tree canopy (birch, pine) with wind protection
- **Avoid:** Exposed sites, south-facing walls, areas near limestone or concrete

## Monthly Care Calendar

### January
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** Leaf curl is normal in extreme cold (moisture protection mechanism)

### February
- **Tasks:**
  - Check for desiccation damage (brown leaf edges)
  - Water on mild days if soil is dry and unfrozen
- **Watch for:** Sun scorch on exposed south-facing plants

### March
- **Tasks:**
  - Remove any winter protection as temperatures rise
  - Check for and remove dead or damaged branches
  - Scratch back mulch to allow soil to warm
- **Watch for:** Emerging flower buds; protect from late frost if needed

### April
- **Tasks:**
  - Apply acidifying fertilizer (rhododendron/azalea formula)
  - Begin regular watering
  - Protect flower buds from late frost with fleece
- **Watch for:** Flowering begins (variety dependent)

### May
- **Tasks:**
  - Enjoy the main flowering period!
  - Deadhead spent flowers immediately after bloom
  - Apply fresh acidic mulch (pine needles, composted bark)
  - Water consistently
- **Watch for:** Lace bugs, vine weevil

### June
- **Tasks:**
  - Complete deadheading
  - Remove any reverted (green) shoots on variegated varieties
  - Water deeply in dry weather
  - Check soil pH if leaves look yellow (chlorosis)
- **Watch for:** New growth emerging

### July
- **Tasks:**
  - Water regularly (rhododendrons have shallow roots)
  - Do NOT prune (flower buds forming)
  - Monitor for pests
  - Stop fertilizing
- **Watch for:** Powdery mildew in humid conditions

### August
- **Tasks:**
  - Continue watering in dry weather
  - Check for vine weevil damage (notched leaf edges)
  - Reduce watering frequency as temperatures cool
- **Watch for:** Flower buds setting for next year

### September
- **Tasks:**
  - Allow natural hardening off
  - Continue watering if dry
  - Apply sulfur if pH test shows soil becoming alkaline
- **Watch for:** Leaf color should remain dark green

### October
- **Tasks:**
  - Deep water before ground freezes
  - Apply thick mulch layer (10-15 cm pine needles or bark)
  - Clean up fallen leaves from around base
- **Watch for:** Prepare winter protection for exposed plants

### November
- **Tasks:**
  - Install windbreaks for exposed specimens
  - Spray anti-desiccant on foliage (optional, for very exposed sites)
  - Ensure good mulch coverage over root zone
- **Watch for:** Secure protection before hard frost

### December
- **Tasks:** None required
- **Watch for:** After heavy snow, gently brush off branches; check windbreaks

## Pruning Guide

### General Principles
- **Rhododendrons rarely need pruning** if properly sited
- **Deadheading is the most important "pruning"**
- **Timing is critical:** Prune immediately after flowering

### Deadheading (Essential)
- **When:** Immediately after flowers fade (May-June)
- **How:** Snap off spent flower clusters at base, above the new growth buds
- **Why:** Prevents seed formation; directs energy to new growth and next year's buds
- **Caution:** Be careful not to damage new growth buds just below flower cluster

### Shape Pruning
- **When:** Right after flowering (June)
- **How:**
  1. Cut back to a whorl of leaves or visible dormant buds
  2. Remove no more than 1/3 of the plant
  3. Make cuts just above a leaf whorl
- **Result:** Plant will branch from just below the cut

### Rejuvenation Pruning
For old, leggy, or overgrown plants:
- **When:** Late winter (March) or immediately after flowering
- **Method 1 (Gradual):**
  - Cut 1/3 of oldest stems to 15-30 cm each year for 3 years
- **Method 2 (Drastic):**
  - Cut entire plant to 30-45 cm from ground
  - Risk: Plant may take 2-3 years to recover and flower
  - Not all varieties tolerate hard pruning—test on one branch first

### What NOT to Do
- Don't prune after July (removes next year's flower buds)
- Don't shear with hedge trimmers (damages leaves, looks unnatural)
- Don't remove more than 1/3 of foliage in one year

## Seasonal Considerations

### Before Spring (February-March)
- Test soil pH (should be 4.5-6.0)
- Order acidifying fertilizer and sulfur if needed
- Check for winter damage but wait to prune
- Plan watering schedule for dry spring weather

### Soil pH Management
Rhododendrons MUST have acidic soil. Signs of pH problems:
- **Yellow leaves with green veins (chlorosis):** Soil too alkaline
- **Solutions:**
  - Apply sulfur to lower pH
  - Use acidifying fertilizer
  - Mulch with pine needles
  - Avoid lime, wood ash, and concrete runoff near plants

### Before Winter (October-November)
- **Hydration:** The single most important factor—water deeply before freeze
- **Mulching:** 10-15 cm of acidic mulch (pine needles, bark) over root zone
- **Wind protection:**
  - Install burlap screens on windward side
  - Or plant evergreen windbreak nearby
- **Anti-desiccant spray:** Optional for very exposed plants
- **Snow:** Can actually insulate; don't remove unless breaking branches

### Winter Leaf Behavior
- **Leaf curling:** Normal response to cold—leaves curl and droop to reduce water loss
- **Uncurling:** When temperatures rise above freezing, leaves should uncurl
- **If leaves stay curled:** May indicate root problems or desiccation—water if possible

## Common Problems in Swedish Climate

### Pests
- **Vine weevil:** Notched leaf edges; larvae eat roots. Apply nematodes in spring/autumn
- **Lace bugs:** Stippled, pale leaves with black spots underneath. Spray insecticidal soap
- **Aphids:** Distorted new growth. Spray with water or soap solution

### Diseases
- **Phytophthora root rot:** Wilting despite moist soil; fatal. Ensure good drainage, avoid overwatering
- **Powdery mildew:** White coating on leaves. Improve air circulation
- **Leaf spot:** Brown patches on leaves. Remove affected leaves, improve conditions

### Nutrient Problems
- **Chlorosis (yellow leaves):** Usually pH too high; acidify soil
- **Brown leaf edges:** Salt damage, drought, or cold desiccation
- **Poor flowering:** Over-fertilization with nitrogen, wrong pruning time, or insufficient light

### Environmental Issues
- **Winter desiccation:** Brown, dried leaves in spring—caused by frozen soil + wind + sun
- **Frost damage:** Brown flower buds or new growth—cover during frost warnings
- **Sun scorch:** Bleached or brown patches on leaves—provide more shade

## Tips for Success

1. **Choose hardy varieties:**
   - Finnish rhododendrons (Helsinki, Haaga, Elviira series) are bred for Nordic conditions
   - Yakushimanum hybrids (compact, very hardy)
   - Species like R. catawbiense and R. maximum

2. **Site selection is critical:**
   - Dappled shade from birch or pine trees is ideal
   - North or east-facing slopes
   - Protected from winter wind
   - Away from concrete, limestone, and drainage from house foundations

3. **Soil preparation:**
   - Test pH before planting
   - Amend with peat moss and composted bark
   - Ensure excellent drainage (raised beds if needed)
   - Never add lime

4. **Planting depth:**
   - Plant at same depth as in pot (shallow!)
   - Root ball should be visible at soil surface
   - Don't bury the stem

5. **Watering wisdom:**
   - Shallow roots need consistent moisture
   - Water deeply but infrequently
   - Most critical: water before winter freeze

6. **Mulch matters:**
   - Always maintain 10 cm mulch layer
   - Use acidic materials: pine needles, composted bark
   - Keep mulch away from stem

7. **Deadhead religiously:**
   - This makes the biggest difference for next year's blooms
   - Do it immediately after flowering

8. **Monitor soil pH:**
   - Test annually
   - Swedish rain can leach acids, but concrete and lime cause problems
   - Acidify proactively if pH rises above 6.0
